## Key Takeaways
- Governance frameworks must explicitly define ownership of quality metrics post-AI deployment
- Accountability structures break down without clear role assignment when AI systems are live
- Quality ownership cannot default to "shared responsibility" without specific decision authorities

## Frameworks / Models

**AI Quality Ownership Model:**
- Define who owns: response accuracy, escalation decisions, knowledge base updates, customer satisfaction impact, incident response when AI fails
- Establish decision authority hierarchy: who approves changes, who investigates failures, who rolls back problematic outputs
- Map governance to existing org structure vs. creating new AI-specific teams

## Guardrails & Anti-patterns

**Anti-patterns to avoid:**
- Vague "shared ownership" between Support Operations and AI/ML teams (creates accountability vacuum)
- Assuming product/engineering owns AI quality because they built it (disconnects from customer experience reality)
- Post-deployment governance neglect ("we'll figure it out once it's live")

## Decision Criteria

**Questions to resolve before AI goes live:**
1. Who investigates customer complaints about AI responses?
2. Who has authority to disable AI for specific issue categories?
3. Who owns SLA compliance when AI contributes to slower resolution?
4. Who audits quality metrics and sets thresholds for human override?
5. Who decides if AI quality regression warrants rollback vs. retraining?
